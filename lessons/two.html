Mandarin Chinese Grammar

In this lesson, we will discuss basic Mandarin Chinese sentence structures. We will learn how to form a sentence and where to find useful grammar-related resources.

Part 1

Chinese grammar is simple. You only need to learn a few grammar structures in order to feel confident in expressing yourself. It's simple at first and as you continue to learn and get more used to it, the simpler it gets. Let's start.

Sometimes a word is comprised of one character like "车" which means a car. Sometimes it is comprised of two for example like "地图" which means a map. It can even have three characters like "小宇宙" which means a microcosm.

Notice for a moment how the characters are stacked together.

"地图" is comprised of "地" which means earth or ground. "图" means picture so you can easily understand the basic idea behind the composition.

"小宇宙" is a word comprised by "小" which means small and "宇宙" which means universe. As a result we have "small universe" or the scientific term for it: "microcosm".

The SVO Formula

The foundation of Mandarin Chinese grammar is built on the so-called SVO formula. Subject+Verb+Object.

See Example:

You drive (a) car - 你 开 车 Nǐ kāi chē

In this example 你 is the subject, 开 is the Verb and 车 is the Object. (There is no "a" or "an" in Mandarin Chinese like there is in English.)

More examples:

You speak Mandarin Chinese - 你 说 汉语 nǐ shuō hànyǔ

You drink water - 你 喝 水 nǐ hē shuǐ

You read (a) map - 你 看 地图 nǐ kàn dìtú

You read (a) book - 你 看 书 nǐ kàn shū

I go (to the) hotel - 我 去 酒店 wǒ qù jiǔdiàn

You come (to the) hotel - 你 来 酒店 nǐ lái jiǔdiàn

These are all basic SVO sentence structures that can be used in various situations. If you want to change these sentences and make them into a question. You'd simply add a 吗 ma character at the end and you change the sentence into a question. It's as simple as that. Look:

Are you speaking Mandarin Chinese? - 你说汉语吗? nǐ shuō hànyǔ ma?

Are you drinking water? - 你喝水吗? nǐ hē shuǐ ma?

Are you reading a map? - 你看地图吗? nǐ kàn dìtú ma?

Are you reading a book? - 你看书吗? nǐ kànshū ma?

Are you going to the hotel? - 你去酒店吗? nǐ qù jiǔdiàn ma?

Are you coming to the hotel? - 你来酒店吗? nǐ lái jiǔdiàn ma?


There are many ways to use the same pattern again and again and still imply completely different things by adding or changing individual characters.


呢 ne is a very useful character and is used by native speakers all the time. it basically means "how about...?" or "what about...?" Let's take a look at more examples:


I speak Mandarin Chinese, what about you? - 我说汉语，你呢？ Wǒ shuō hànyǔ, nǐ ne?

I'm drinking water, how about you? - 我喝水，你呢？ Wǒ hē shuǐ, nǐ ne?

I´m reading a map, what about you? - 我看地图，你呢？ Wǒ kàn dìtú, nǐ ne?

I´m reading a book, how about you? - 我看书，你呢？ Wǒ kànshū, nǐ ne?

I´m going to the hotel, what about you? - 我去酒店，你呢？ Wǒ qù jiǔdiàn, nǐ ne?

He´s coming to the hotel, what about you? - 他来酒店，你呢？ Tā lái jiǔdiàn, nǐ ne?

Part 2

If you meet a stranger and you want to ask him who he, she or what it is, there's is a simple formula to follow.

Who are you? 你是谁？Nǐ shì shéi?

Who is he? 他是谁？Tā shì shéi?

Who is she? 她是谁? Tā shì shéi?

What is it? 它是什么? Tā shì shénme?

Notice above how the words for "he", "she" and "it" sound the same, but have different characters.

Maybe you want to know who's stuff it is or who's pet this cat is that you found on the street. You can use the same formula and add the particle 的 de, in order to clarify possession. Look here:

Who's client are you? 你是谁的顾客？Nǐ shì shéide gùkè?

I am your client. 我是你的顾客。Wǒ shì nǐ de gùkè

Who's boss is he? 他是谁的老板？Tā shì shéide lǎobǎn?

He is your boss. 他是你的老板。Tā shì nǐ de lǎobǎn.

Who's tour guide is she? 她是谁的导游？ Tā shì shéide dǎoyóu?

She is your tour guide. 它是你的导游。Tā shì nǐ de dǎoyóu.

Who's car is it? 它是谁的车? Tā shì shéide chē.

It is the company's car. 它是公司的车。tā shì gōngsī de chē.

Part 3

To have or not to have, that is the question. But how to express it in Mandarin Chinese? That's an even more important question. You simply use the verb 有 yǒu. Here we go:

I have a car. 我有车。Wǒ yǒu chē.

I have a phone. 我有手机。Wǒ yǒu shǒujī.

I have coffee. 我有咖啡。Wǒ yǒu kāfēi.

I have a client. 我有顾客。Wǒ yǒu gùkè.

I have a tour guide. 我有导游。Wǒ yǒu dǎoyóu.

If you don't have these things you simply add a 没有 méi yǒu and then you can express that you don´t have them.

I don't have a phone. 我没有手机。Wǒ méiyǒu shǒujī.

I don't have coffee. 我没有咖啡。Wǒ méiyǒu kāfēi.

I don't have a client. 我没有顾客。Wǒ méiyǒu gùkè.

I don't have a tour guide. 我没有导游。Wǒ méiyǒu dǎoyóu.

The word 不 bù is also used to express the negative. In order to use it, you have to put it in front of a verb. Look here:

I don't drive. 我不开车。Wǒ bù kāichē.

I don't drink coffee. 我不喝咖啡。Wǒ bù hē kāfēi.

She is not my client. 他不是我的顾客。Tā bùshì wǒ de gùkè.

He is not my tour guide. 他不是我的导游。Tā bùshì wǒ de dǎoyóu.
